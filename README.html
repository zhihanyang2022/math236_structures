Proposition

Defn. A proposition is a sentence that is either true or false, but not both.

Ex.

  • 2 + 3 = 4, P, truth value: False
  • I have two children, Q, true value: True
  • “This statement is false” can’t be assigned a truth value, so it is not a proposition,

How to combine 2+ propositions to create a new one? Using logical connectives.

Conditional

A conditional logical connective is also a way to combine propositions, in addition to the ones listed above.

Defn. A conditional logical connective says this: does a combination of P and Q contradicts if P then Q?

  • P: hypothesis / antecedent
  • Q: conclusion / consequence

Truth table.

Ex. Suppose P is “I kicked the ball” and Q is “the ball moved”. Then the truth table above translates to:

  • Does "I kicked the ball" and "the ball moved" support the if-then?

    • Yes.

  • Does "I kicked the ball" and "the ball did not move" support the if-then?

    • No.

  • Does "I didn't kick the ball" and "the ball moved" support the if-then?

    • Well, at least it doesn't contradict it - so yes.
    • Explanation: "Perhaps a strong gust of wind moved the ball, or a toddler came along, while you were drinking water, and kicked the ball.” (Stackexchange Math)

  • Does "I didn't kick the ball" and "the ball did not move" support the if-then?

    • Well, at least it doesn't contradict it - so yes.

Biconditional

A biconditional logical connective is also a way to combine propositions, in addition to the ones listed above.

Defn. A conditional logical connective says this: does a combination of P and Q contradicts P iff Q (P happens whenever Q happens, and P fails whenever Q fails)?
